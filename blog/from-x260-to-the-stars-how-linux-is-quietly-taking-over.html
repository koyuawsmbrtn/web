From X260 to the Stars: How Linux Is Quietly Taking Over

Published on April 29, 2025

I'm lounging here with my trusty ThinkPad X260, running Sway as my window manager and Kanshi seamlessly managing my display profiles. Everything operates smoothly, no bloatware, no intrusive telemetry, no unnecessary clutter. It's just me and my system, tailored to perfection. The hum of the fans is barely noticeable, the keyboard worn smooth from years of dedicated service. This machine, simple and sturdy, is more than enough for everything I need, and that's the magic.

And now? The world is catching on.

PewDiePie, one of the most popular YouTubers, recently made headlines by switching to Linux. In his video titled "I installed Linux (so should you)", he documents his journey from Windows to Linux Mint and eventually to Arch Linux. Instead of struggling, Pewds handled his Arch setup with surprising skill, setting up Hyprland as his window manager and even writing his own scripts and dotfiles. Watching him confidently tweak his system was almost cathartic; it's proof that even the biggest names can thrive when freedom is on the table. His honest excitement, the customizations, the new workflows, the empowerment, mirrors what so many Linux users experience when they realize they are no longer trapped by corporate design.

Similarly, James Lee, a renowned animator, has transitioned to Linux, citing the freedom and efficiency it provides for his creative work. In discussions on platforms like Reddit, users highlight his move as a significant endorsement of Linux's capabilities for professionals in creative fields. (Source) His switch reflects a growing sentiment among artists and developers who are fed up with closed ecosystems and subscription traps. It's not just a tech thing anymore; it's a lifestyle choice, a rejection of unnecessary complexity and corporate gatekeeping.

While others grapple with system bloat and restrictive ecosystems, we navigate our self-built systems with ease. It's not about being edgy or "tech elite." It's about reclaiming ownership. Your data, your system, your rules. It's about understanding that technology should serve you, not the other way around. This isn't just a trend; it's a cultural shift. One built on autonomy, transparency, and genuine craftsmanship. One built on the simple idea that creativity thrives best where control and freedom are given back to the user.

Linux isn't taking over in a loud, headline-grabbing explosion. It's seeping in quietly, reshaping how people think about technology. It's in the indie game developers ditching proprietary engines. It's in the digital artists breaking free from expensive subscription models. It's in the students, the researchers, the musicians, all building, sharing, collaborating without artificial barriers.

Meanwhile, Windows, the system literally named after "windows," continues to offer some of the worst window management imaginable. Windows users find themselves constantly dragging, resizing, rearranging, and fighting their workflow, trapped in an outdated paradigm that feels increasingly clumsy compared to the elegant, dynamic tiling of Sway, Hyprland, and other modern Linux environments. It's ironic: the platform that made "windows" its core metaphor still doesn't know how to handle them properly.

And me? I'm still here, lounging with my Sway setup, Kanshi managing my displays, a satisfied grin, and a front-row seat to the future. Watching as the seeds we've planted for years finally break through the concrete.

Because make no mistake: the future is open. And it's already here.
